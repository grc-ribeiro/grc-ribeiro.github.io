GRC 322: Assignments

Designers posters (final)

With what you learn from your work and my feedback, you want to work on a final version of your 12″× 18″ poster.

You need, however, to write a document explaining your design decisions.

Why?

To design is to make decisions. During the exploration phase, you tried different solutions. By analyzing the solutions, you learn what was successful, what wasn’t, and why. Now that you’ll make the final decision for the project, you’re better informed and therefore better prepared to make the decisions. Making the decisions, however, take some thinking, and that’s when writing helps. If you force yourself to write reasoning before making a decision, your reasoning will tend to be stronger, and your decisions will tend to be more rightful. To write is to think.

Evaluation

You will not be evaluated on your layout, but on your ability to make design decisions and explain them.

Your grade will be 80% based on your explanation of why each of the decisions that you made is superior to the other options that were investigated in the process. Make sure that what you’re describing as your final solution matches your final solution.

The remaining 20% will be based on the formatting of the document itself.

  • Use of the grid

    20 points

    Why did you decide on this specific grid (number of columns, margins, and gutters)? What changes did you make on the from last week’s layouts? How did you improve the use of the grid since last week? Did you make good use of the grid’s flexibility while still keeping a grid? How did you align images and typography to the grid and to each other? How does the blank space work in relation to the elements in the layout?

    • 20 pts Satisfactory
    • 15 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 10 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Typeface selection

    20 points

    What type families did you choose? Why? Why were they a better choice than the others you considered? If you chose to work with two or more type families, how do they complement each other? Conversely, if you chose to use only one type family, how does it support enough variation?

    • 20 pts Satisfactory
    • 15 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 10 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Typographic decisions

    20 points

    What is the line-length of the main paragraph? Why did you decide on this? What’s the line-length of the captions? Why did you decide on this? What about leading? How did you decide on the leading? And other typographical decisions such as type size, weight, and color for the title and the quotes?

    • 20 pts Satisfactory
    • 15 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 10 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Images

    20 points

    What were the big challenges with the images? How did you decide on what to include and what not to include? Did you group the images based on the type of work, on color balance, or any other criteria? How did you decide on what images should be bigger on the layout? Were you constrained by the images’ pixel resolution? If so, how did you overcome such limitations?

    • 20 pts Satisfactory
    • 15 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 10 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Document typography

    20 points

    Use as many pages as you need in your document (probably somewhere 1½ and 2½ pages), but make sure your typography is excellent. Don’t try to be original, just try to be good, but remember: “default is defunky!” You want to use a nice typeface, with nice leading, margins, line‑length, etc. You want to use proper hierarchy for title and heading. Control the rags and widows. And if you center any heading or subheading or if you use inappropriate typefaces, it’s an automatic zero for the document typography.

    • 20 pts Satisfactory
    • 15 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 10 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

Tasks

  1. Watch the video critique and read my comments from the first round.

  2. Work on your final layout for the poster.

  3. Open your text editor of choice (Word, Pages, Google Docs, etc. I use plain TextEdit) and write your reasoning for any decision you made

  4. Go back to step 2, then step 3 again. Iterate until you’re happy with your layout and the reasoning behind your decisions.

  5. Check spelling and grammar. You can use Grammarly, for example, but I think that Google Docs or MS Word work just as well.

  6. Paste the text in a new letter-size document in InDesign.

  7. Format it, with a nice typeface, leading, line-length, proper hierarchy, etc. Format it with as much care as if it was your resume or a cover letter. Better yet, design it as if your advanced typography professor was going to evaluate your type choices, because I am.

  8. Export as a PDF.

  9. Export the poster as PDF files with and without the grid.

Submission

  1. Name the PDF file of your poster without the grid as lastname-final-grid-hidden.pdf.

  2. Name the PDF file of your poster with the grid as lastname-final-grid-show.pdf.

  3. Name the PDF file of your written document explaining the decision‑making process as lastname-design-decisions.pdf.

  4. Submit all three files to Canvas.

Please check Canvas for deadline.
