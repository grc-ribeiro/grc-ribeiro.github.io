I would also like to learn about open use vs. licensing fonts as they apply to the workplace. When can I use Adobe Fonts? If a client is requesting a logo, can I choose any one that I have a license to or do they have to pay as well?

Do you automatically determine the classification of typefaces in your head when you see them?

Sort of. Not exactly, but I do recognize some characteristics of each style. The problem with the classifications is that they’re incomplete and artificial. But they’re an interesting device to point to differences in type families.

I did not know that there were subsections to types, such as the 4 subsections of Blackletter.

There are. And noticing their differences is more important than knowing their specific subclassifications.

I am still a little confused about how to identify these subcategories, but I’m sure this will come with practice.

Yes. You’ll learn to notice the differences between groups and subgroups and between typefaces within the same subgroups.

I chose to make all my characters 4 pixels wide. Because the “v” needs to be pointy at the bottom to differentiate it from a “u,” I struggled to make the point fit within the 4-pixel width. I ended up compromising by making the “v” asymmetrical.

If Susan Kare designed an asymmetrical ‘v’ for the Apple Computer’s typeface in 1983, I guess we don’t even need to call it a compromise.
