I really struggled with having my first header line up with my lists and other hierarchy. I was wondering if there was a better and faster way.

Yes, there is! But it’s a secret. Only those who actually read this journal highlight will know. You probably noticed that 1em didn’t mean the same in all elements. Because em is relative to type size, when you increase your type size, it increase the relative unit em. Let’s see… You always want to keep your paragraph type size as 1em to respect your user’s preferences, so its type size will be the same as what we call the root em or rem. But when you make the type size of h2 as 2em, your new em for your h2 is twice as big as the em for the paragraph. So if your margin for p is 1em, you need the margin of h2 to be 0.5em to keep them properly aligned. That’s pretty simple math. But let’s say your margin for p was 0.75em, and the type size for your h2 was 1.4em. In this case, if you want them to be aligned, the margin for h2 would be 0.75÷1.4em, which is 0.53571429em. The math is not complicated, but you can see how it gets inelegant fast. So here is the trick: You may use rem for margins and paddings, to keep them always relative to the root em. In this case, my margin for p would be 0.75rem and my margin for h2 would be 0.75rem too! Not only does it eliminate the use of math (you’re welcome), but also makes it visually clear that they are the same. I hope it helps.

Margin creates an area around an element, while padding creates space between the element’s content and its border.

Correct.

Use em for measurements instead of px, since this is a common best practice for flexible sizing.
