Last week, I came across the Studio Regale website that is interactive in a very unique way. It uses sound effects and encourages the visitor to interact by clicking anywhere on the page. I wouldn’t say it’s the most user friendly, but it does make the experience memorable (thanks, Von Restorff).

That’s the trade-off. Almost twenty years ago, I prototyped a Flash website for my studio that you could navigate with mouseovers and no clicks. I showed it to some friends and the consensus was that it was cool to play with, but too confusing, so we never launched it.

For example, I realize that I always shop at the same small grocery store because I subconsciously know that if I were to shop elsewhere, I would get overwhelmed by the number of new choices and don’t have time for that decision-making.

Exactly. It’s comforting.

When I come across a form that doesn’t appear to require much information at first, but then reveals many more input fields on a following page, I usually leave the form and don’t come back. While I was working on the content hierarchy assignment, I realized I didn’t really know how to semantically section off elements. I noticed you used article and aside, but I wasn’t really sure how to use those correctly (if there is a correct way), so I defaulted to using section. Do you have any tips on how we should go about grouping elements and with what tag?

You want to make sure they have the proper semantic value. I believe the excellent MDN website usually provides the best explanations.

While uploading my HTML/CSS: Content Hierarchy assignment my menu hyperlink was not working. I was really confused because in Brackets the link worked fine but not when I copied the codes to GitHub. After half an hour of troubleshooting, I realized that in the codes for my menu hyperlink I used a capital ‘M’ instead of a lowercase ‘m’ which made the entire hyperlink not work.

Please use only lowercase for file names, links to file names, tags, classes, etc.

The wayfinding example with the lines on the ground of the office space can also be confusing for people with color-blindness. Subway map makers on this topic recommend using patterns on the lines in conjunction with colors.

It can be confusing to those with daltonism or other forms of color deficiency. But I believe most of those who have any color deficiency would distinguish the saturated colors on such solutions.
