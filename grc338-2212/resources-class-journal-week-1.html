Class journal, week 1

I’m not exactly excited to be back in a coding-based course, but thus far my expectations have been exceeded greatly!

Let’s talk again in week 9. You may be surprised by how this class goes.

I learned the structure of the class and got an understanding of what I need to do each day!

Yes. Class will be very consistent.

I liked the video you made summarizing the design principles, especially explaining the examples and providing feedback; it was good review.

I plan to post one every Friday. And I expect everybody to watch them.

I enjoyed your review of our examples. It was very enlightening in many ways. Personally, I learned more about the Aesthetic Usability Effect by watching the video and comparing our examples to others. I found the Crispy Carrie chocolate bar example incredibly informative as to a more accurate example of the Aesthetic Usability Effect.

It was a good example, indeed.

I learned checking examples with you prior to submitting is a smart move (saved my team some deductions, for sure).

Smart (and easy) move, indeed.

The first time I copy/pasted the code into the index, I didn’t copy it correctly or missed something, so the code wasn’t working which was really frustrating. When I redid the step though, it worked… must have missed an extra space or something.

I’m glad it worked after a second try. In the future, if it doesn’t work after a few tries, you can ask for a colleague to look at it, or you can send me an email with screenshots too, of course.

Group discussion wasn’t difficult. I found that linking a google doc is helpful so that everyone can contribute and/or work on it at the same time.

Any group can use whatever tool you agree upon. Google doc is a great option that mixes some of synchronous and some of asynchronous in the right amount.

I really appreciated you taking the time to comment on our discussion; this made the assignment more clear.

Any time you need my help with anything, just let me know. You just have to work ahead of the deadline.

The examples I saw listed for the design principles discussion were all super interesting. Rarely do I ever take the time to read through a whole discussion, but I felt encouraged to do so in this group!

Good to know! I found the examples really good overall.

Failure is GOOD. It’s an opportunity to learn and improve a product or process.

Yes. Failure is only a real failure when you don’t learn anything from it.

Eventually, I would love to explore techniques for maximizing the ease-of-use and perceived friendliness of our work.

We’ll cover it in this class, but it’s also covered in GRC 339 and GRC 429.

In watching the video, I also learned that the focus of this class will be on the user, rather than on a consumer. I like this focus because it will hopefully allow us to concentrate on the actual quality of our work, rather than trying to market it or whatnot.

You got it!

I learned that wireframes are detailed, extremely early stage prototypes.

That’s how I use the word “wireframe” at least. Others use them differently, though.

Are there specific elements (ie- color, photos) that are universally applied in web design to increase the aesthetic usability effect?

Nope. But blank space and consistent alignment help a lot.

I didn’t know there were many variations to prototyping. Prototyping means the creation of a simple design or idea. I had no idea that there were different types of prototyping including concept prototyping, throwaway prototyping, evolutionary prototyping and incorporate prototyping.

More important than knowing their classifications it to understand that you want to build the prototype for what you need to learn.

I still sometimes have a hard time distinguishing between the 3 basic structures of hierarchy because some examples appear that they can be more than 1 basic structure. Is it possible for hierarchy to be represented with more than one structure?

Yes. Absolutely.

I was assigned to provide examples for the aesthetic-usability effect this week, which was really interesting for me because it put something into words that I had already formed an abstract understanding of in my mind. Basically, learning more about this effect helped to affirm some of the instincts that I’ve been feeling for a while now.

You’ll notice it more times in the next week, that instinctively you knew most of those principles to be true, but you didn’t know their names. Knowing their names and having discussing them you help you make more intentional decisions in the future. To design it to make decisions.

One thing I’m still a bit confused with is the relationship between prototyping and iterating. Is it correct to say that a prototyping cycle before a final release is “iterative prototyping”?

I can’t see why not.

What defines a complex hierarchy, how much is too much for a tree structure?

There’s no hard rule. You have to figure it out on your own or (ideally) through usability tests with real users.

While reading I understood the distinction between the three types of hierarchy when reading. However, I couldn’t identify what type of hierarchy it was when I was looking for examples for the group assignment. Similarly, the rest of the group found the distinction between the tree, nested, and stair hierarchy to be difficult when they go one-deep in levels. Layla, one of my group members, shared an article that helped cleared our confusion.

I’ve included the article in the readings. (Thanks for sharing, Layla!) But don’t worry about the distinction between different hierarchy representations. Just keep in mind that you have to aim at having the best visual representation for the content hierarchy.

Something this week that stood out to me was the article, “Why is so much design school a waste of time?” This was really encouraging for me to read. I always forget that as a creative designer, I am a gold miner, not a customer. Being a good listener isn’t needed in just your close relationships but in every aspect of life. When it comes to designing and working with clients, I want to become skilled in listening for that 10% of important information.

It is a great article. When Professor Cezzar first shared it on Twitter, I promised her I’d assign it to all my future classes.

On my own did some relearning of some HTML on LinkedIn Learning.

That’s cool! They tend to be super helpful and well done.

I finally remembered the shortcut to a Mac Screenshot and the proper capture techniques.

I beg everybody to follow them. I put a lot of effort to make my lectures/videos well presented. I need good images to go in them.

I read through the instructions for submitting work on GitHub for the rest of the quarter which was confusing, but I’m hoping that doing the actual process will clear things up.

Like everything else in this class, GitHub is simpler than it first seems. But if you need my help, I’m here to help everybody succeed.

My understanding from this week is that there is a compromise in designing, where everything initially intended in the concept may not be included for sake of overall functionality and efficiency.

To design it to make decisions.
