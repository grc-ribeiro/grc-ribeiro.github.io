GREP cheat sheet

Here are some tips for using GREP.

  1. To start, got to Edit > Find/Change

  2. To search for a letter, you want to use \w. It’s a wild card for any uppercase letter, any lowercase letter, and the underscore (_). But you will find just uppercase letters if you search for [A‑Z] or just lowercase letters, if you search for [a‑z].

  3. To search for a number, you want to use \d. It’s a wild card for any digit, from 0 to 9.

  4. For spaces, you may just use a space.

  5. If you want to search for one or more repetition, you want the +, sign. For example, \d+ will search for any sequence of consecutive numbers.

  6. If you want to search for zero or more repetition, you want the *, sign. For example, \d* will search for any sequence of consecutive numbers or no numbers at all.

  7. If you want a specific number or range of repetition, you can use curly brackets. For example, \d{4} will look for a sequence of four digits; \d{2,5} will look for a sequence from two to five digits; and \d{7,} will look for a sequence of seven or more digits.

  8. Some symbols—such as ., ‑, and ?—have special meanings on the GREP syntax, to search for them, then, you need to backslash them first. For example if you search for ., you will find any character at all, but if you search for \., you will find a period.

  9. When you use the square brackets ([ and ] ), you can search for any character in the bracket. For example, [bcf]at matches the words bat , cat , and fat , but not hat or rat.

  10. The caret means not; for example [^b]at will match cat and hat, but not bat.

  11. The search with bracket can be combined with repetition. For example, [\w\d]+ will search for any sequence of letters and numbers.

  12. To use a captured group, you need to capture the group with parenthesis and use them later with the dollar sign ($1 for the first captured group, $2 for the second, etc.)
