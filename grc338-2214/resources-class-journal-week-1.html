About the Aesthetic-usability effect, which is that “Aesthetic designs are perceived as easier to use than less-aesthetic designs.” (I had known/ encountered the idea of what looks good being perceived as easier to use, but was unaware that there was a term for the phenomenon).

This will happen a lot with the principles. You may have noticed some of those phenomena, but you didn’t know their names. And one you learn their names, it’s easier for you to make and to explain your decisions. To design is to make decisions. You want to have good rationale for them.

As a designer, I want to investigate further into aesthetic usability effect and see how this plays out in my own work in this class.

Another way to think about the principle is that users tend to perceive ugly things as harder to use. (And untrustworthy.)

I really liked the example of the use of aesthetics on the TurboTax website because that is most definitely a stressful and tricky process that anyone would be overwhelmed by. I like the concept of the aesthetic-usability effect. It gives relevance to our design work and incentive to always try and make things look better.

I really like this principle for the exactly same reason!

My group had the topic of iteration; I never really knew that there was a difference between an iteration and evolution. Prior this this week, I thought that iterations could be created with changes in time.

They can. But that’s not what the principle was about. So it’s important to really understand the principle beyond its title’s meaning in the dictionary or other contexts.

One question I do have about this is, would you consider prototypes and iterations the same and if not, how do they differ? Could you say that a prototype is an iteration of a product?

A series of prototypes in the same phase of prototyping are iterations. For example, for each of the backpacks in the video, we only saw one paper prototype, but I’m sure the designers tried different iterations (wider, thicker, longer flaps, etc.) before moving on the next phase.

Some concepts that are confusing to me still are the basic ways to visually represent hierarchy: trees, nests, and stairs. I understand the concept and why it is used on paper but if I were to do this/make one, I’d have some trouble forming the relationships.

I think you’ll intuitively find the best solution to convey your hierarchy.

The evolution of a product/webpage is not considered part of the design iteration process. Iterations focus on repeating a set of operations until a specific result is achieved. This usually comes from testing multiple ideas and choosing the one that best meets the design requirements.

Precisely.
