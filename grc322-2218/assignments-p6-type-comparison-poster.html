GRC 322: Assignments

Type comparison (posters)

Indvidual assignment. Sit away from the students who were in your group in module 3.

Design three different 24″×36″ posters that inform about the differences and similarities of the type comparison webpage you worked on in module 3. Include some facts and a brief history of each typeface, but emphasize similarities and differences.

Make sure to pay attention to  all the details on typography that we have covered in this class and you have learned in GRC 318, including: Typeface selection, leading, line length, kerning, tracking, grid, baseline grid, type anatomy, type hierarchy, indentation, hyphenation (just turn off), widows, orphans, proper punctuation characters, old-style figures, etc.

Since this is the exploration phase, you want the posters to be very different from each other.

Tasks

  1. Choose the best graphics to effectively show the similarities and the differences between the typefaces.

  2. Design a 24″×36″ (portrait) poster, using a flexible grid, include some brief history of each typeface, but emphasize the comparison.

  3. Design another 24″×36″ (portrait) poster with a very different solution for the same prompt.

  4. Design a third 24″×36″ (portrait) poster with yet another very different solution for the same prompt.

Requirements

  1. Format

    24″ wide
    36″ tall
    Horizontal posters are an automatic zero.

  2. History of each typeface

    For each typeface, include facts such as who design it, when, and where. Also, explain if there was a specific purpose for its design and the historical context of its creation.

1. Comparison
---------------------
Explain, through images and diagrams, what are the biggest similarities and big differences between the two typefaces assigned to your group.

1. Style
----------------
For the main text use a typeface that is very different from both typefaces you are researching. Suggestion: if your typefaces are sans-serif, use a serif typeface for your copy; and vice-versa.

(Don’t include the visual references.)

Style tips

  1. Just as on the webpage, make sure your lines don’t get longer than 60 characters.

  2. Turn-off hyphenation.

  3. Check for widows.

  4. You don’t want indentation on your poster. It’s always either indentation or skipping a line. On a book, you use indentation; on a poster, you skip a line. But never both.

  5. Check your hyphens and dashes.

  6. Don’t use default fonts. You probably have access to Adobe Typekit, use it.

Evaluation

  • History

    10 points

    Are the facts context for each typeface properly described and explained on the page? Did you include type designer, year, and location? Did you try to set political and artistic context in which the typeface was designed and released?

    • 10 pts Complete
    • 8 pts Partially complete
    • 5 pts Incomplete
    • 0 pts No information

  • Comparison

    30 points

    Did you do a thorough comparison of the typefaces, including uppercase, lowercase, punctuation, and numerals? Is the comparison effective? Would someone be able to distinguish them after reading your webpage? (Somewhere between twelve or twenty images of comparison at least.) Thorough comparison, including uppercase, lowercase, punctuation, and numerals. One would be able to easily distinguish both typefaces after visiting the page.

    • 30 pts Complete
    • 20 pts Partially complete
    • 10 pts Incomplete
    • 0 pts No information

  • Typography

    20 points

    Are the typefaces appropriate? Does the visual hierarchy reflect the content hierarchy? Do the paragraph lines have an appropriate length? Is the type size adequate for legibility? Is the leading adequate for the type size?

    • 20 pts Satisfactory
    • 15 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 10 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Grid

    10 points

    Are all the elements properly aligned to the grid? Is the grid being well used to convey planning, intelligibility, clarity, orderliness, and credibility? Are elements very well aligned within the grid making the layouts organized but also visually interesting? At the same time, does it show enough flexibility of the grid?

    • 10 pts Satisfactory
    • 8 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 5 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Color and Consistency

    10 points

    Is color being used effectively to direct users to attract attention, group elements, indicate meaning, and enhance aesthetics? Are similar parts related? Are related parts similar? Are the variations consistently applied in all parts of each poster? (Posters don’t need consistency in the set, only within each layout. Don’t even think of them as a set, they are supposed to be different; see next criteria.)

    • 10 pts Satisfactory
    • 8 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 5 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Exploration

    20 points

    Did you explore enough variation between layouts? Did you explore different colors, type families, grids, image sizes, and placement, etc.? You don’t need different type families for every single layout, but you want to explore different ideas.

    • 20 pts Satisfactory
    • 15 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 10 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

Submission

  1. Export each of your layout twice, once showing the grid, and once without it.

  2. Make a 3‑page PDF document with all the pages exported, with the grids hidden. Name it using the name convention lastname-type-poster-grid-hidden.pdf.

  3. Make a 3‑page PDF document with all the pages exported, with the grids showing. Name it using the name convention lastname-type-poster-grid-show.pdf.

  4. Submit both files to Canvas.
