GRC 322: Resources

Class journal, week 4

I like how in this project, we got to both practice applicable skills with the grid layout, and learn about design history! That was neat.

That’s one of my favorite assignments of the academic year for this very reason.

The grep challenge we did in lab this week was quite difficult for me to figure out because we were never shown how to write dashes in grep. Are we allowed to google questions like that during lab or should we just ask you for help instead?

You are always encouraged to google anything in my labs.

I had some trouble getting the vertical grids to show, but I realized I needed to change the view threshold to a smaller number.

Yes. Or you can just zoom in to see it when you need to.

I also realized that I don’t really enjoy making a lot of versions of things. Rather than helping me vary my ideas, it more so jumbles my thoughts and makes refining actually harder for me. I prefer to create a few very different designs, and refine from there.

Well, I guess it counts as learning too. I don’t want you to think that there’s only one design process. You’ll find yours.

I wish we got more peer feedback about our posters during lab.

We’ll have more collaboration next week, when you’ll be working on your final poster. At this time, the goal was to generate ideas. At this phase, you don’t to over analyse them. Now it’s time to analyse your poster to its fullest!

Depending on the grid layout, designs can begin to look more interesting. Using a grid doesn’t mean you should stack all your elements perfectly in rows/columns.

That’s correct. Break the predicability of the grid.

White space should be used with purpose, not just because you don’t know what else to do.

That’s a true statement.

I was looking for a bit more information about grids so I found this link: https://uxplanet.org/grids-in-graphic-design-a-quick-history-and-5-top-tips-29c8c0650d18.

Thanks for sharing, Natalie

I’m interested in learning how to select good typefaces for titles, since I always want to use fun ones but never know how to find them.

Fonts In Use is a good start. You can also browse first on Adobe Fonts, and then find out more about the type family before using it.

I recently learned about the ch unit in CSS. I tested it out on a webpage paragraph block restricting the width to 60ch (the recommended line length from what I’ve heard), but it feels too narrow when viewing on a desktop. What are your thoughts on using this unit to optimize web reading?

I think I heard about it a few years ago and didn’t pay much attention. I found this article by Eric Meyer, whom I trust a lot. He didn’t seem to care for it.

I was surprised that I had never really used a baseline grid before. I am not sure why. There have been many times where I would require that sort of text alignment and would resort to much less efficient methods.

Well, now you know.

I was wondering if you have any tips on how to be more intentional with the use of white space? Is it mostly about balancing the design, or are there other things you generally take into consideration? Thank you!

It is about balancing. But it’s also seeing the blank space as an active element of the layout. Pay attention to its shapes.

If you make the leading of a specific paragraph style 2/3 of the baseline grid and align only its first line to the grid, every third line after the first line of the paragraph will be sitting on the baseline grid.

Precisely. And that makes your type look neat!

Sometimes following the grid so closely makes for a boring design—you can create something unexpected to the grid.

I know what you mean, but you can still follow the grid closely and create something unexpected with it.

This week I learned a lot about utilizing baseline grids. I’ve only used columns to align my elements before.

You’ll never design without them again, right?

Using grids is helpful in designing and makes everything look nice and organized. They’re great.

Yes, they are.

When I use a grid, my design looks legit.

Yes, but only if you use it well.

I overthink the GREP exercises a lot.

You all do.

I used GREP in book design this week which was rewarding to apply the concepts we are learning in this class to another! It is super helpful for cleaning up the text file we are working with. It also takes a lot of practice to understand how GREP works and the “codes” per say to apply when needed. At the end of the day it is just a formula to understand.

You’re right. Took me a while to learn it too. Now I use it every day. Several times a day through different scripts, actually.

I feel like all of the six poster designs I submitted still need lots of refinement for me to be happy with them.

And that’s why we have another lab to work on it now. But you’ll submit only one in the end.

After learning about baseline grids, I feel like I’ve unlocked a big level in InDesign. I’m excited to start using baseline grids on my future InDesign projects now!

And I look forward to seeing your best work.
