GRC 322: Resources

Class journal, week 6

I’m interested to know how long other people took on to create their three type comparison poster, just so I can benchmark myself. I feel like it took me a really long time to get through them… probably 8–10 hours on the three.

I’d say you’re definitely on the longer side.

This weeks assignment was my favorite one yet, comparing typefaces is very ~aesthetically pleasing~.

You’re my people.

This week’s lab really stressed me out since I felt like I didn’t walk out with drafts/layouts I really liked. I definitely feel a bit burnt out from being back in person and sitting in a lab for almost 3 hours. I’m glad that at least our lectures are virtual and asynchronous.

Please take breaks.

I really liked looking at the examples of the typographic posters. Something that I found interesting is one of the posters which had angled content. When doing that on InDesign, would you design according to a grid and then angle everything at the end? Or can you angle grids? Do you like this kind of design?

I think that kind of design can be really effective. It’s a little tricky to do it in InDesign, though. In Illustrator, it’s easier to either draw diagonal lines and make them guides or to select guides and rotate them. But I don’t think you can make an automatic grid just by choosing the number of columns and the gutters width in either application; you’ll need to build the grid with guides, which is not that hard actually.

In a departure from the last assignments, I did research on type comparison posters and sketched out ideas before starting in InDesign. Although I ended up deviating from my sketches (the scale of the actual poster makes a huge difference), they still gave me inspiration for pieces of the poster.

Sketching layouts on paper never helped me very much, but I know of several designers who always sketch their designs before starting working on the computer.

One tactic I used today in lab that helped me work more efficiently, was gathering all of my design and informational elements prior to working on any layouts. This method made it easy to switch out elements and whip up new poster designs.

I like that too. I just add everything on the poster. Then, I need to make it look good.

The two posters I spent the longest on in class ended up being my least favorites.

It happens. And they’re not necessarily the worst solutions; maybe they haven’t achieved their fullest potential.

Working with the grids really helped with starting off the type poster assignment. I find myself working on details more rather than layout so thank you for giving me that heads up during class! I found it really helpful working in increments of 40 minutes—time crunches really help me!

I want you all to learn different processes. I’m glad it worked for you, but it may not work for others, but now they know.

I’m looking forward to working in groups to create the final poster. I really like the critiques because not only do we get constructive feedback from a fresh eye, but also a look at everyone’s different styles of designing and organizing content.

Yes. It benefits who gives the feedback and who receives it.

Getting an understanding of grids in the Designer Poster assignment prepared me for this next one, where I can use more columns and be bolder with my choices; it’s almost as if you’ve taught this before?

Yes. It even feels like I know what I’m doing, right?

I was unsure how large to make my type for the research part. I settled on 22pt, but I think that may be too big.

Sounds big. We’ll check it in the lab next week.

I’m wondering if doing the gutter math is absolutely necessary; I like to eyeball it after doing the math.

Not super necessary. I just like to do it to use round numbers later. It helps me.

Out of curiosity, what’s your opinion on right aligned type? I don’t remember if you’ve mentioned it before.

It’s a quick way to make your type look amateur.

The Kleene Star and the Kleene Plus metacharacters essentially represent either 0 or more or 1 or more of the character that it follows. I thought Lesson 7 was pretty cool and I thought the names were easy to remember since it’s so unique.

I actually didn’t know their names until then.

I am interested to see how it will go working with my group after working independently this round. It will be interesting to see the variety in layouts and ways of explaining the differences between the two typefaces. I am hopeful that their perspectives will help us create a super strong poster.

I’m sure they will.

I found designing such a large poster on a small laptop to be a bit difficult at times, as it was hard to visualize how the design will actually look once scaled up to its printed size. Command 1 lets you zoom your page to view it at the real size, which I found to be helpful while designing my posters.

We’ll look at them at 1:1 next lab, with the projector.

Infographic design is something I’ve been wanting to practice, but it’s pretty difficult in reality. This type comparison poster assignment reinforces how much thought needs to be put into the content and visualization. An infographic poster design course would be a cool GrC class to have... Maybe have the prerequisites be GRC 322 and GRC 404?

Information design and data visualization are topics that I’m very passionate about. Maybe an idea for a senior project? We’ll talk.

Are there any particular design trends that you’ve noticed for typographic posters in the industry now? Is there a certain style that’s becoming more or less popular?

That I don’t know. Not sure if there’s enough of typographic posters in the industry to create a trend.

Would love to see how you would make one of these posters!

I have no idea how it would end up being, but it’d certainly depend on what typefaces I’m comparing. The design would change based on both the most informative method of comparison and the most appropriate visual language for the typefaces I’d be working with.
