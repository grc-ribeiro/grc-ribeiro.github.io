Class journal, week 2

I struggled a lot with the descender for the g for the six-pixel font height and was deciding whether legibility in a bigger size or smaller size mattered more. Ultimately I zoomed out and opted for better readability when it was small. For the six-pixel font, would you say that’s the better approach?

Yes. Absolutely.

My hypothesis was moderately correct at least for some regarding the 12 height being subjectively more difficult because of having so many more options. Mine ended up being pretty similar to my 9 height but without some of the sacrifices, which I guess was kind of the point.

With more possibilities, more decisions need to be made. That’s why we’re working on pixel font and not with vector. A “real” font would be too hard for you to design and for me to advise too.

The concept behind GREP is pretty darn cool. A decade or so ago, I might’ve been very curious about how detailed items, like books or databases, were made and edited. Throughout my secondary and post-secondary education career, between things like GREP, Latex, coding, and automation, it is satisfying to be able to answer many of those childhood questions.

Markdown is your next step.

Pixel font helped show that it is important to step away from your work and come back with fresh eyes.

Yes. It’s often helpful.

With typefaces (and by extension, designs in general), it is sometimes necessary to make compromises from the original due to constraints… such as maximum pixel height.

True.The trick is to embrace the constrains rather than fighting them.

The last thing I want to mention is that the peer reviews and feedback on my pixel fonts was refreshing because it helped me take a step back and view my fonts from an outsider’s perspective.

It’s so good to be back in the lab!

I took your advice from the video critique video and looked up some pixel fonts online for inspiration. That helped me a lot when I was figuring out how to do the E and S!

That’s always a great idea. Look up your references.

I learned more about GREP this week, notably that there is an actual difference especially from a GREP standpoint between option + ; and … as a use of ellipses.

No! That’s just good typography. If you want proper ellipses (…), you need the proper character, which on the Mac, you get with option+;.

Learning how to use GREP is extremely helpful especially considering I am in the book design class and we need to know how to use the GREP shortcuts like . and \s and \r. These are all very helpful in fixing type errors throughout a long string of text without having to do it all manually.

Yes, you’ll need it in GRC 439.

This GREP reference chart was helpful: http://www.ericagamet.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/04/Erica-Gamets-GREP-Cheat-Sheet.pdf.

Both Nicole and Stephanie shared this same PDF from Donna’s Book Design class. Thank you both!

When replacing three dots with proper ellipses in GREP, you only use a “\” in the “find” input and not in the “change to” input.

That’s a true statement.

When using GREP, don’t escape periods (.) or other special Regex characters (e.g. \^}[ etc.) in the “Replace” field, but do use the Regex syntax for whitespace characters (e.g. \s\r\t).

Correct. I don’t make the rules, though.
