Class journal, week 5

Paula Scher is awesome. Definitely a new inspiration of mine.

As a pioneer among women in a field that was so male dominated (and sadly still is more than it should be), she might be the most important design influence in the United States as, in most programs, somewhere between 60% and 80% of the students are young women hoping to be as successful as she is.

Out of the designers you assigned us, which one is your favorite?

That’s a tough one. I don’t really like to choose a favorite. But I’ll go with Elaine and Saul Bass. Shout out to Layla for pointing out how important Elaine’s work was. We always hear about Saul’s work as if she was not his co‑designer. But I choose them because of their wide range of work, from logos to movie title sequences.

I was a little confused about how I should lay out the document for the design decisions paper but I decided simple was the best way to go about it. I hope it doesn’t backfire on me.

Let me check… You did great on the document formatting.

I was unable to see the annotated PDF with comments for the designer poster. I tried viewing it through Canvas and through Adobe Acrobat, but had no luck. I just used the comments I gathered from the video critique.

It’s not annotated on the PDF, just comments on the assignment.

Learning more about my designer makes me want to read everyone else’s posters.

I’ll share a PDF with all of them combined.

Chaining my keyboard increment to the same size as my baseline proved to be helpful when making adjustments. It can be a bit tedious to try to align everything just using your cursor.

Great suggestion!

I think I said this before, but I need to use baseline grids more often. I probably hesitated to use them before since they seemed complicated and appeared to do a bunch weird stuff if you don’t understand how they work.

Please do understand and use them.

Being able to justify your design decisions is also extremely critical. Designing with the reasoning behind your choices will allow for the creation of designs that are more intentional and usually more effective as well. Overall, I ended up enjoying this project in the end – taking the time to actually sit down and articulate what decisions I made in my design process and why helped make me more cognizant of the intention of each element in my design and allowed me to improve areas of the design that were lacking adequate justification for their incorporation.

To design is to make decisions.

Although it is important to iterate different ideas of design and layout, it is just as important to explain design decisions and understand our own reasoning in making the choices we did.

Again, to design is to make decisions.

Having to explain my design decisions will allow me to design mindfully in the future.

To write is to think.

I feel like I was able to be bolder with my design choices this week. Last week, I think I was kind of “playing it safe,” although I did not realize it at the time. This week I was able to move past that and utilized my grid much better as a result.

I’m glad it worked in the end. But what I hope is that you learned not to play safe in the first rounds. Be bold! My children are dancers, and one thing that they hear a lot is to go full out on every move until the director says it was too much. Same goes for design too. I had students going too far and then I told them to hold back, but I’m glad they tried it.

I found myself enjoying the explanation process; normally, designing is done silently and so it was really nice to put my thoughts to paper. You find the intention in everything and if there is no intention, then you need to go back and make a decision, because that’s what design is—decision-making.

You got that right!

I enjoyed reading this short piece about the inspiration behind Susan Kare's command key design. She's simply a genius. Here you are: Iconic designer Susan Kare explains how ⌘ came to be

Thanks for sharing, Ryann! And yes, she is indeed a genius.

The decision-making process of design is extremely important – experimenting and analyzing the solutions you are able to come up with will help you learn what is successful, what wasn’t, and why.

You got it.

There is always growth to be had! Peer and instructor feedback is crucial to understanding that growth and being pushed to better design.

Exactly. When you move fast, you can have feedback on more things. Then, you don’t sweat the details of bad ideas. (You do very much sweat the details of your final product, though.)

This assignment felt a little backwards to me. We rushed to place everything at the beginning with little time to think about conscious decisions and then had to redo everything with intention. Maybe this is how it will work in real world jobs but it definitely was an adjustment for me.

Not backwards. First, you generate ideas, without thinking too much about them. Then, you make conscious decisions. The almost chaotic first layouts help you inform the decisions you made in the second round.

I got a lot of good ideas that I probably wouldn’t have gotten if I was just alone in my room designing a poster.

You’re probably right. I’m glad we’re back in the lab.

It was nice seeing the progress of everyone’s poster and fixing a lot of the little mistakes many in the class did as well.

Having students learning from each others’ mistakes is only one of the things I’ve missed from the labs.

I find it easier to break a grid with an odd number of columns compared to an even number.

It can be, especially on a poster. But in a magazine project, for example, it can make the process of using the system more complicated.

I have confirmed that I don’t want to do layout for the rest of my life.

Fair enough. But you have some layout for the rest of the quarter at least.

At first I didn’t get the necessity of the baseline grid but now I see how helpful it is and I’ve already used it on projects for other classes.

It makes it more organized. And also, it makes it easier to make decisions.

I found that when you align items to the baseline grid, the functions of using ex. space before and after were limited. I am having trouble understanding when you should align elements to the vertical baseline grid and when it is not necessary.

Nothing is “necessary”, baseline grid makes it look better. And you don’t want more options to add space before or after the paragraph. Baseline grid makes the process easier and produces better results.

I learned that I need to practice adding more white space into my designs — I tend to try to fill up all the open space like a puzzle and end up cramming too much stuff in. I learned captions can be really helpful in adding in this white space.

The captions can be a nive change of rhythm too.

The design decisions write-up was helpful because it forced me to be intentional about every decision by providing reasoning for it. Your feedback and also the group feedback helped me with the poster, because after spending so long staring at the poster I was starting to doubt everything I was looking at.

This feeling never goes away. Make sure you always have a someone you trust to give you feedback, even if you’re working alone.

After seeing some of the type specimen examples in GRC 453, I’m excited for the process to get to that level for the final project!

You can start thinking about what type family you’ll want to feature. It needs to be one that was designed in this century.

And I was able to make some good headway on the things I felt I was struggling with last week!
