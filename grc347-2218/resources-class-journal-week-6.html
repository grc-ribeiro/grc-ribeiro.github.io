GRC 347: Resources

Class journal, week 6

It’s hard trying to design a unique packaging shape that stands out but also doesn’t detract from its functionality.

Your packaging shape doesn’t need to be unique.

While researching materials, it’s difficult to find one that is environmentally friendly, able to match the properties of plastic, and is inexpensive.

It is hard. If it wasn’t, everybody would use it.

A basic principle in design including packaging is that if something is designed well, you should not need instructions to use it.

That’s actually a popular oversimplification. Not all products must be intuitive, for complex systems with a specialized audience, training is necessary and therefore guessability is not as important as learnability. (Look it up.)

A couple of weeks ago, I started using this new script editor. Still trying to learn the shortcuts, I wanted to switch between a couple of different JSON files that it had open. Given a common mental model for interacting with tabs, I did Command-2 and, lo and behold, it switched me to the second opened script. Such software, alongside packaging, includes a lot of good examples of these affordances, such as with different browsers or Adobe applications.

Mental models are very powerful.

For the example on affordance, I was a little confused at first about what you meant by the shopping bag and scissors’ shapes inviting us to interact with the object. The light switch was more straight-forward because of the labels written on it, but I am guessing you meant the big open shape of the scissors and handles of the bag that would cause interaction?

Yes. You know how to use them.

I want to make sure that my packaging uses the principle of form follows function, but I can feel myself thinking about form and aesthetic first just because of the original idea I had for my product. I will continue to push myself to prioritize functionality where it makes sense instead of just incorporating things because they look nice.

Yes. Don’t detract from functionality. Apple for years removed ports from its laptops frustrating its users that wanted HDMI or SD card readers. Last week, they brought it back. The new Macbook may not look as sleek as the previous model, but it’s a better computer.
