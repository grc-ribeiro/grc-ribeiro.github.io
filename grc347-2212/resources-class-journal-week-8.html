You can have the perfect idea of what you want to do, but physically making that look is still difficult. No matter what anyone says, design is not easy.

Who said it was?!

I really appreciated getting feedback from my classmates about my iterations. It influenced the final design I went with.

I’m glad it worked.

I thought the Market Pantry example was very unique in showing a product line despite having a wide variety of typefaces and layouts used.

It’s quite remarkable how they achieved it if you think about it.

I realized while working this week that I really liked this slow, drawn out process of making the packaging by first establishing a brand voice, mission, name, feel, etc. before starting the actual design process. It makes for a more structured creative space where it’s easy to rule some ideas out and easy to build on others because you know what kind of message you’re trying to send and what you’re working towards. When you start with designing packaging right away, there’s essentially infinite possibilities because the brand doesn’t have any voice or essence yet that you’re trying to capture.

You got it!

I think that the idea of products in a line being somewhat similar and somewhat different from each other is common sense, but only because it is so effective that it’s been instilled in our minds as a result of our interaction with commercial products.

Sometimes, we forget to apply common sense to our own designs.

https://www.mockupworld.co/ is a great resource I found to apply your graphics onto some (free) mockups!

Thanks for sharing, Mikaela!

Something I’m working on for the project is detail and information for labels. Thinking about what else should be on the labels besides the brand and product details, such as bar codes or recycling symbols.

Please, make sure to research what’s required.

The most common strategy for consistency and similarity on packaging is to have the same size and shape for each kind of product, with different colors for each flavor and scent.

Accurate.
