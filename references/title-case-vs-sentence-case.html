A case for sentence case on all titles and headlines

Title case or headline style is the capitalization of almost all words in a title or headline. The rules vary a little from different style guides1, but the idea is that you capitalize the first and last words in a headline, and then every word except articles, coordinating conjunctions, and preposition with fewer than five letters. Nouns, pronouns, adjectives, verbs, adverbs, and subordinate conjunctions should be capitalized. Depending on how comfortable you are distinguishing subordinating from coordinating conjunctions, the rule may or may not sound confusing to you. Either way, it certainly feels arbitrary.

Title case is used in the New York Times, the Wall Street Journal, Time Magazine, and the Chronicle of Higher Education. It’s also the preferred style of many online publications, including A List Apart, Design Observer, Under Consideration, and the blogs of both Jonathan Hoefler and Tobias Frere-Jones. Furthermore, it’s on the title and headings in “The Elements of Typographic Style” no less. So if you have always used title case on your titles, and never thought about changing it, you’re clearly in good company.

If you think, however, that virtually every publication in the world uses title case, you could not be farther from the truth. The style seems to be very rare outside the United States. Most newspapers in Canada, Australia, and the United Kingdom2 use the simple sentence case on their headlines. Capitalization in their titles follows the same rules of any sentence in the publication. Sentence case is also used by many American publications, such as the Boston Globe, the Los Angeles Times, and the USA Today. Finally, the style is recommended by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO). So I guess you’re not short of allies either if you choose to use sentence case on your titles.

Sentence case is simpler to implement since you don’t have to think too hard about the crazy, arbitrary rules of title case. You’ll be, then, more likely to be consistent. My preference is obviously biased, since I grew up in Brazil, where all publications use sentence case. But to this day, I still find it confusing to read headlines in title case. It simply doesn’t flow well. It doesn’t look good to my eyes. It also unnecessarily conveys a sense of self-importance that I try to avoid as much as possible. I’m aware, though, that those are all subjective reasons that come down to personal preferences.

My anecdotal self-reporting data isn’t, however, an isolated and extreme case. If you’re writing on the internet, you may be read by anyone in the world, and most people are only used to sentence case and may be confused and slowed down by title case. That doesn’t mean the opposite case can be problematic, though. Those (mainly in the U.S.) that are very used to title case are also used to sentence case, because that’s how they read the vast majority of their sentences anyway. You’re not excluding or even favoring a group of readers if you opt for sentence case. You’re choosing to be internationally accessible.

Speaking of which… Do you know what else is more universally accepted and highly recommended by the ISO? The metric system. But I’ll get to it on another day.
