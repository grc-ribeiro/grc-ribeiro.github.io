GRC 339: Assignments

Final website mobile first

For this assignment, you need to create a website for a news publication. This publication has a specific focus rather than broad general news or even topics such as fashion, sports, or music. Think specific genre of fashion or music, or maybe a specific league or even a team.

Examples of focus for the publication:

  1. Vegetable home growing
  2. Young women in business
  3. American street art
  4. Underground hip hop
  5. NBA
  6. Manchester United
  7. Bird watchers
  8. Calligraphy and lettering
  9. etc., etc, etc.

Please, don’t limit yourself to the options above. They were just examples.

Tasks

  1. Download the HTML and CSS files, and work on the homepage, the page for one story.

  2. Make sure to include a very simple logo. I’d suggest its name on a nice typeface and one color. (With that said, you want to set the logo as an image on a PNG file, though.) This is not a class on logo design; you won’t be evaluated on the originality and effectiveness of the logo. Just make sure it won’t be a distraction to your web design work.

  3. Include a navigation with four to nine sections of the publication.

  4. On the homepage, you want to include 8 to 12 stories, with at least 4 images. You may use placeholder text for now. (Lorem ipsum.)

  5. Link the main story on your home page to another page with the story. You may use placeholder for the text (except the headline), but you need three or four images on the page.

  6. Please follow additional instructions in the comments of the HTML files.

  7. Use final colors, images, style, etc. But keep it simple.

  8. Style your website using one single CSS file, making sure it looks appropriate on a smartphone. (You don’t need to worry about other screen sizes for now; it’s for smartphone sizes only. But you want to add a max-width:XXem for your div.container.)

  9. Style your website, but be very economical and follow these CSS restrictions.

  10. You may use fonts from Adobe Fonts or Google Fonts, if you want to. (I recommend Adobe Fonts, because of their in‑house excellent type team and their deals with many other also excellent type foundries.)

  11. Do not use any grid or flex property for now. Don’t use float either.

Requirements

  1. Two pages: a homepage and one story, with tentative final style and at least four images per page. (Placeholder text is fine for now.)

  2. Navigation with at least four sections. (Please use proper navigation markup as you did in GRC 338.)

  3. Don’t forget to include the header

  4. When you’re done, use tools such as FreeFormatter, HTML Formatter, or Code Beautify to organize your HTML and CSS code.

  5. Only one CSS file for all the pages. (Not one for each page.)

  6. Check the list of easy mistakes to avoid from GRC 338.

Evaluation

Beyond your technical skills on the HTML and CSS files, you will be evaluated on your capacity to apply the design principles that we have discussed in class to your work, using the rubric below.

Design principles rubric

  • Aesthetic-usability effect; Form follows function; Expectation effect

    Were aesthetic decisions on the website guided by the appropriate language for the business it’s representing and its functionalities?
    10 points

  • Alignment; Hierarchy

    Does the placement of elements create a sense of unity and cohesion, contributing to the design’s overall aesthetic and perceived stability? Does the alignment of elements correctly lead the user through the page? Does the visual hierarchy represent the content hierarchy?
    10 points

  • Entry point; Visibility

    Does the home page conveys its job as the initial point of the website? Does it promote additional interaction? Are the main information and functionalities on each page clearly visible to the user?
    10 points

  • Color; Highlighting; Von Restorff effect

    Is color being used effectively to direct users to attract attention, group elements, indicate meaning, and enhance aesthetics? I’d suggest using tools such as  Adobe Color CC to achieve a harmonic color palette.
    10 points

  • Consistency; Similarity

    Are similar parts related? Are related parts similar? Are the variations consistently applied in all parts of the website?
    10 points

  • Picture superiority effect; Face-ism ratio

    Are images being used to complement the information on the website? If you have images of people, is the appropriate face-ism ratio being respected in all images?
    10 points

  • Proximity; Horror vacui

    Are related elements close together? Are close elements related? Are the amount of blank space on the pages appropriate for the business the website represents?
    10 points

  • Legibility; Chunking

    Is the text legible? Does the text have appropriate size, typeface, contrast, line-length, and spacing? Is the information broken into appropriate chunks, so it is easier to process and remember?
    10 points

  • Mental model; Wayfinding; Iconic representation; Fitts’s law, Serial position effects

    Is the layout consistent with users’ expectations? Can users easily navigate through the pages of the website? Are the buttons easily understandable? Do the links follow a logical order? Are the buttons easily selectable, even if users are using their fingers on a touch screen rather than a mouse?
    10 points

  • Hick’s law; Progressive disclosure

    Are the possibilities of interaction limited to an appropriate number at each page? If there are too many options, are they broken into easily identifiable divisions in the page?
    10 points

 
Other principles, such as Recognition over recall, Errors, Forgiveness, and especially Accessibility must be taken in consideration, but they are covered by other principles and/or by the HTML/CSS part of the evaluation.

Tips

  1. Try to reuse classes for consistency (if you need them at all).

  2. Make sure to use appropriate, semantic tags and class names.

  3. Use proper heading tags for headings, proper paragraph tags for each paragraph, appropriate list and list items tags for the lists, etc.

  4. Make sure to use appropriate, semantic property values in your CSS.

  5. It’s mobile only. If you’re even thinking about using two columns, take a look at your phone and imagine your content using less than half of the phone’s width. Design with your work open on the browser’s mobile built‑in simulator.

  6. Don’t forget to test in different phone sizes.

  7. Make sure to include the <meta name="viewport" content="width=device-width, initial-scale=1.0"> tag to have your page rendering correctly on mobile phones.

  8. Don’t change the hierarchy of your HTML based on how the pages render on the browser. “I don’t like the bullets” is not a good reason not to use lists or list items; you can style the bullets out in CSS. “I want the navigation to be bigger” is not a good reason to make them headings; again, just style it in CSS.

  9. Color palette: Start with one (very) light gray for the background, one (very) dark gray for the text, and one accent color. Add more colors as you need them.

Groups

This assignment was designed to be an individual project. But since some of you are already collaborating with another student, I want to encourage you keep collaborating. Therefore, you may choose between groups of one or two students. But there are three simple rules.

  1. If you choose to go solo, don’t complain of the amount of work. The assignment was designed to be accomplished by one person.

  2. If you choose to work with someone else, don’t complain about your teammate not working as much as you are. The assignment was designed to be accomplished by one person, and the choice of working with someone else is yours.

  3. Either way, you need to sign up with your group of one or two people on Canvas.
