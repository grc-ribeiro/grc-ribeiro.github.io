Sounds like you’re overthinking it.

I think one of the best components of the typeface comparison assignment for me was learning about the history of each font. What struck me as most interesting was how old a lot of popular fonts are, as well as the reason why they were created.

And you will get to read everybody’s page.

It is crazy how some typefaces can look so similar upon first glance but then actually have so many differences. I suppose that is the whole point of this course, but it is very cool to sit down and actually dissect each character.

It is, indeed, very much the point of this assignment.

Our group submitted our font comparison assignment a little bit early, thank you for putting the time in to tell us the places we could improve so that we had the time to adjust before the final due date.

No problem. But ideally you want to check rather than submitting it.

Towards the end of the alphabet like the last 7 letters it’s almost as if the designers got lazy since our two fonts are almost identical.

Make sure to show this on your comparison.

I found the pixel typography project more difficult than expected. I think it was a good experience overall, seeing how I can continue to refine and perfect something, even past my own expectations. I think that the limitations and constraints made the project more interesting and made it a better learning experience overall. 10/10 would recommend this project for anyone wanting to learn about typography.

Thanks for writing this. When I was planning the class, I almost axed it. But I think it went well. I don’t think we need the grays, though; I would make 6, 9, 12-pixel heights instead. And I’ll make sure to write more clear description.

Group collaboration can be challenging but very rewarding. It doesn’t change after school. It’s not easy to meet with my colleagues in the department, but it’s always rewarding to work with such smart people.

It’s too bad that papers like the New York Times and the Wall Street Journal still use title case in their headlines, but it’s good to see that the Boston Globe and L.A. Times have moved to sentence case, increasing readability.
