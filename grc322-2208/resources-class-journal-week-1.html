Class journals, week 1

Can I just say how much I appreciate how your classes are formatted? Everything is planned out, there isn’t a bunch of busy-work, everything is concise and to the point, and I feel like I’m prepared to take the class even though it’s only week one. :).

Thanks, but like you said, it is only week 1. Please let me know if at some point things are not that great anymore

I found the history of typography and printed type to be so precise and nicely condensed in the video! I think that it touched on many important points while not lingering too long on the details.

Always my goal with the videos.

The main point of the history of typography is the history of the democratization of information.

Yes!

It was refreshing to watch the videos on the history of type, because although this is definitely not the first time we’ve all seen this information, it has been a while and was a good refresher (especially with how concise it was).

I figured it could be helpful to quickly refresh your mind. Also, I like to emphasize the point that technology is supposed to bring democratization of resources (such as information) instead of increasing the social gap.

The dot of the I is called a tittle (what a fun word).

It is fun word, but if somebody at a party call it a dot, please let it go. Don’t be that person.

While I learned typography terminology in some of my other classes, it was a great refresher. I am glad that you mentioned the detailed terminology. I would love to be familiarized with them enough to where I don’t have to be studying for a test to know those terms, but rather I use them as a tool to help me describe type and the effective use of those elements.

The best way to learn any vocabulary is to use it them when you need it. It won’t be on the test, though.

For the six-square height font, I have not figured out how to do a legible G. I think the hardest letters are the E and S. They both need a decent amount of squares to begin with just in order to make the letter form legible plus they have to be consistent with the other letters’ design.

That’s the tough part of the 6-square font. The e and the s wouldn’t be so hard if it wasn’t for the descender of the g.

For this assignment, I had difficulty knowing when to move on to the next letter/design and when to step away to take a break since you want to perfect everything.

Very good points. Working on type design requires a lot of macro/micro shifting.

The pixel font assignment has been a good way to break down the structure of type and how the characters flow together. As I experiment more with hand lettering, I will think more critically about how color and structure impact the legibility and readability of a typeface.

You got the learning objectives of the assignment exactly right.

The pixel project was initially frustrating because I felt very limited, especially working within a 6 pixel height. I also found it hard to know what was meant by adding two greys. I know that greys are typically used to soften curves but with a 9 pixel height, it didn’t really soften anything so I opted to make shadows and “gradients.” I wish the instructions were a little clearer on that (maybe some examples?) but nonetheless, I’m still interested to see how I’ll edit these iterations further.

You got it right on your first take. The grays are meant to soften the curve. I don’t like to give examples for this assignment because it leads to too much copying of the examples. But I agree that, since the class is online, the instructions could be more clear. I’ll clarify some points before the next submissions.

Since starting my internship and file prepping art from other designers, I have added a LOT more fonts to my library that I am excited to use. Any tips for organizing fonts/licenses (personal vs. commercial use)?

I use Font Explorer, but because I got a license from Cal Poly. Not sure if there are more affordable alternatives. Please let us know if you find out.

I was working on the UX/UI for a forum over the summer, and when it came time to find a typeface to use, it turned out to be one of the most difficult portions of the project because there are so many that look similar that it was hard to choose. I think it just takes a little more experience and practice but I still hope to learn about what goes into a typeface that makes it unique from others.

Yes, with experience, you will learn to pay attention to details.

I would love to investigate typography of non-latin characters. I think that there are worlds of design that I don’t have access to because of language barriers. Studying character based languages and the typography associated with them would be cool.

Oh, tell me about it. I wish I knew other languages just so I could appreciate other kinds of typeface design. It’s really hard to appreciate their efficacy without any context of the language. I just find them beautiful.

Met with Bruno to discuss Pixel Font requirements, but gained a stronger understanding on the values of legibility, consistency, and readability.

I highly recommend meeting with me during my office hours.

I haven’t heard of it before. I’ll take a look.
