GRC 322: Resources

Class journal, module 1

Ask questions when you don’t understand the point of an assignment/task.

Please do.

I was very intrigued by the Karate Kid speech in the beginning of class. I have never heard this before and found it to be very interesting.

It’s famously (or infamously), a hallmark of GRC 338.

Bruno structures his classes quite similarly, which makes it easy to get settled.

I do. I hope it helps. I think it does.

Watching the evolution of type reminded me slightly of the ‘simplification’ phenomenon people have observed with brands/logos.

That’s an interesting point. Also, pictograms. Everything needs to be a simplified pictogram when a rich illustration could be at least just as effective and probably more fun.

I think making the pixel fonts was fun and I’m excited to do the next ones but it also gave me eye strain.

I’m glad you had fun. But I’m sorry about the eye strain. I do understand it, though. Maybe taking some breaks can help?

It was challenging working with a width of 4 pixels, especially making an asymmetrical “v,” but satisfying once I found something that worked.

The asymmetrical “v” is quite nice once you get it.

It was nice to start with a simple / mechanic design for the pixel font and then add more character to it once the structure was there. Most of the character came from removing pixels.

It makes sense. I’m glad you found it nice.

Design requires thinking outside the box.

Not necessarily. Sometimes, you have to think inside the box.

The more pixels you grant a design, the more room you allow for details.

And more decisions you’ll have to make.

Working within constrains and limitations of the pixel grid encouraged creative problem-solving.

Yes. Contrains spark creativity.

It is helpful to have friends in class to ask for help, shout out to Keilana for helping me out by explaining using ascenders and descenders in the grid.

It sure is helpful. But some of your friends in the class you haven’t met yet.

Thank you Sadie for sharing how to make my lowercase v four pixels wide.

And that’s why we have in‑person labs.

Good design does not need to be super creative/inventive/unique.

No, it doesn’t need to be. But it often is, because that’s how you make it better. So when Paul Rand said “don’t try to be original, just try to be good”, it didn’t mean that you can’t be original. Originality will be a byproduct of improvement, not the other way around.

Could you upload the transit map video?

Yes, of course. I’ve put a link in the readings page.

Here’s a cool article by this girl who designed a pixel font from scratch: https://medium.com/@reshay/type-design-pixel-fonts-5eec34c0dd31

Thanks for sharing, Keilana!

I did not realize how many subgroups there was within each big group of type.

There are. And more important than memorizing their names is paying attention to the differences between the type families within the same group or in different ones.

I learned that in Linotype the lines of type are called slugs.

They do. And one of my favorite students, Umika, made one for me that reads “We don’t talk about Bruno”. (But don’t tell Umika that I call her one of my favorite students.)

It seems that the typefaces from SLO street signs can be classified as Blackletter, but I still think the designer could have chosen better.

I agree. Actually, it would be hard to choose worse.

It was already difficult enough to design 8 glyphs of type with just a 9-pixel height, I don’t even want to think about making a full vector typeface.

You really don’t. With all the letters, ligatures, numbers, punctuation, symbols, and marks, a modern font often has over 1,000 per style (not counting the variation within each type family, such as bold, italic, condensed, etc). I don’t mean to discourage anyone from pursuing a career in type design, but I will say that you’re crazy if you choose such career path.

Kerning doesn’t need to be equal between each letter. Actually, it looks quite off when it is. Same thing goes for the width of type. Zoom out and see what looks good to the eye and don’t rely on numbers alone.

Yes. You want it to look right, not go by numbers.

The pt size is the distance between the ascent line and descent line.

Correct.

I’m inspired by the final type booklets we’ll be making and can’t wait to make one!

You have to eat your vegetables before the dessert, though.

I’m not exactly looking forward to the font that’s 6 pixels tall because I bet the A and E are going to be difficult to make lowercase. The one that’s 12 pixels tall looks fun though, and I’m excited to give that one more personality.

The 9-pixel font was the easiest. And you’re right about the a and the e.

Designing a subway map is extremely time-consuming. I don’t want to design a subway map, it seems very complex and scary.

Really? That’s the kind of material I’d love to work on at some point.
