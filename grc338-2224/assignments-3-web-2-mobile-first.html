GRC 338: Assignments

Website redesign: mobile first

Using your HTML files from last assignment, style your website using one single CSS file, making sure it looks appropriate on a smartphone. (You don’t need to worry about other screen sizes for now; it’s for smartphone sizes only.)

You can make your browser window really narrow, to avoid worrying about other sizes. Even better, you can use the mobile built‑in simulator, also called User Agent, in your favorite browser.

Use final content, colors, images, style, etc. But keep it simple.

You want to use fonts from Adobe Fonts or Google Fonts. (I recommend Adobe Fonts, because of their in‑house excellent type team and their deals with many other also excellent type foundries.)

You may want to design the logo of the company, but keep it very simple. I’d suggest its name on a nice typeface and one color. (With that said, you want to set the logo as an image on a PNG file, though.) This is not a class on logo design; you won’t be evaluated on the originality and effectiveness of the logo. Just make sure it won’t be a distraction to your web design work.

Tasks

  1. Start by reading my feedback from previous assignments, so you don’t make the same mistakes.

  2. Replace your placeholder images with actual images.

  3. Review your alt attributes. See if the images are still what you have planned before. (It’s okay if you changed your mind about the images, just make sure the alt attributes are correct.)

  4. Style your website, but be very economical and follow these CSS restrictions.

Requirements

  1. Four pages with the tentative, final content.

  2. Menu with a total of at least twenty items, in at least three different categories. (That’s the actual menu for food, drink, etc., not navigation.)

  3. Each item on the menu needs description and price.

  4. At least one image per page (besides the logo).

  5. When you’re done, use tools such as FreeFormatter, HTML Formatter, or Code Beautify to organize your HTML code.

  6. Only one CSS file for all the pages. (Not one for each page.)

Evaluation

Penalties in the list of easy mistakes to avoid are 20 points now. Three or more mistakes is an automatic zero, with no further comments. There’s no reason not to avoid all of them, though.

Beyond your technical skills on the HTML and CSS files, you will be evaluated on your capacity to apply the design principles that we have discussed in class to your work, using the rubric below.

Rubric

  • Aesthetic-usability effect; Form follows function; Expectation effect

    10 points

    Were aesthetic decisions on the website guided by the appropriate language for the business it’s representing and its functionalities?

    • 10 pts Satisfactory
    • 8 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 5 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Alignment; Hierarchy

    10 points

    Does the placement of elements create a sense of unity and cohesion, contributing to the design’s overall aesthetic and perceived stability? Does the alignment of elements correctly lead the user through the page? Does the visual hierarchy represent the content hierarchy?
    Tip: If you have a centered heading for left‑aligned text, you’ll get a 0 for this criterion.

    • 10 pts Satisfactory
    • 8 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 5 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Entry point; Visibility

    10 points

    Does the home page conveys its job as the initial point of the website? Does it promote additional interaction? Are the main information and functionalities on each page clearly visible to the user?

    • 10 pts Satisfactory
    • 8 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 5 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Color; Highlighting; Von Restorff effect

    10 points

    Is color being used effectively to direct users to attract attention, group elements, indicate meaning, and enhance aesthetics?
    Tip: I’d suggest using tools such as  Adobe Color CC to achieve a harmonic color palette.

    • 10 pts Satisfactory
    • 8 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 5 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Consistency; Similarity

    10 points

    Are similar parts related? Are related parts similar? Are the variations consistently applied in all parts of the website?

    • 10 pts Satisfactory
    • 8 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 5 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Picture superiority effect; Face-ism ratio

    10 points

    Are images being used to complement the information on the website? If you have images of people, is the appropriate face-ism ratio being respected in all images?

    • 10 pts Satisfactory
    • 8 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 5 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Proximity; Horror vacui

    10 points

    Are related elements close together? Are close elements related? Are the amount of blank space on the pages appropriate for the business the website represents?

    • 10 pts Satisfactory
    • 8 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 5 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Legibility; Chunking

    10 points

    Is the text legible? Does the text have appropriate size, typeface, contrast, line-length, and spacing? Is the information broken into appropriate chunks, so it is easier to process and remember?
    Tip: If you have a centered paragraph, you’ll get a 0 for this criterion.

    • 10 pts Satisfactory
    • 8 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 5 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Mental model; Wayfinding; Iconic representation; Fitts’s law

    10 points

    Is the layout consistent with users’ expectations? Can users easily navigate through the pages of the website? Are the buttons easily understandable? Do the links follow a logical order? Are the buttons easily selectable, even if users are using their fingers on a touch screen rather than a mouse?

    • 10 pts Satisfactory
    • 8 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 5 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

  • Hick’s law; Progressive disclosure

    10 points

    Are the possibilities of interaction limited to an appropriate number at each page? If there are too many options, are they broken into easily identifiable divisions in the page?

    • 10 pts Satisfactory
    • 8 pts Mostly satisfactory
    • 5 pts Partially satisfactory
    • 0 pts Unsatisfactory

Other principles, such as Recognition over recall, Errors, Forgiveness, and especially Accessibility must be taken in consideration, but they are covered by other principles and/or by the HTML/CSS part of the evaluation.

Tips

  1. Try to reuse classes for consistency (if you need them at all).

  2. Make sure to use appropriate, semantic tags and class names.

  3. Use proper headings for headings, proper paragraph tags for each paragraph, appropriate list and list items tags for the lists, etc.

  4. Make sure to use appropriate, semantic property values in your CSS.

  5. It’s mobile only. If you’re even thinking about using two columns, take a look at your phone and imagine your content using less than half of the phone’s width. Design with your work open on the browser’s mobile built‑in simulator.

  6. Don’t forget to test in different phone sizes.

  7. Make sure to include the <meta name="viewport" content="width=device-width, initial-scale=1.0"> tag in the head of all your HTML files to have your page rendering correctly on mobile phones.

  8. Don’t change the hierarchy of your HTML based on how the pages render on the browser. “I don’t like the bullets” is not a good reason not to use lists or list items; you can style the bullets out in CSS. “I want the navigation to be bigger” is not a good reason to make the links headings; again, just style them in CSS.

  9. You may want to check out some CSS tips with some tricks.
